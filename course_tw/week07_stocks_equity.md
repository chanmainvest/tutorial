### Section 1: What Are Dividends?

**[VISUAL: Simple definition card]**

**Horace:** So, what exactly is a dividend? A dividend is a portion of a company's earnings paid out to shareholders — usually on a quarterly basis.

**Stella:** Think of it like this — when you own stock in a company, you're a part owner. And some companies reward their owners by sharing profits directly.

**Horace:** Not all companies pay dividends, though. Young, high-growth companies — think tech startups — tend to reinvest all their earnings back into the business.

**Stella:** But more mature companies — utilities, consumer staples, financials — often pay steady, reliable dividends.

**Horace:** Now, dividends are typically paid in cash, deposited directly into your brokerage account. But some companies offer something called a DRIP — a Dividend Reinvestment Plan.

**Stella:** A DRIP automatically uses your dividend payment to buy more shares of the same stock — which is great for compounding over time.

### Section 2: Key Dividend Metrics

**[VISUAL: Dashboard showing key metrics]**

**Stella:** Let's talk numbers. When evaluating dividend stocks, there are a few key metrics you want to know.

**Horace:** First up — **dividend yield**. This tells you how much a company pays in dividends relative to its stock price.

**[VISUAL: Formula card]**

```
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Stock Price × 100%
```

**Stella:** So if a stock pays $4 per year in dividends and trades at $100, the yield is 4%.

**Horace:** Simple enough! But here's the catch — a very high yield can actually be a warning sign.

**Stella:** Exactly. If a stock price drops significantly while dividends stay the same, the yield looks high — but the company might be in trouble. This is called a **dividend trap**.

**[VISUAL: Illustration of dividend trap with falling stock price]**

**Horace:** Next metric — **payout ratio**. This shows what percentage of earnings is paid out as dividends.

**[VISUAL: Formula card]**

```
Payout Ratio = Dividends per Share / EPS × 100%
```

**Stella:** A payout ratio under 60% is generally considered healthy. Above 80%? That might be unsustainable.

**Horace:** And the third key metric — **dividend growth rate**. Has the dividend been increasing year over year?

**Stella:** Companies that consistently raise their dividends are called **Dividend Aristocrats** — they've increased dividends for 25 or more consecutive years.

**[VISUAL: List of Dividend Aristocrat examples]**

### Section 3: Dividend Investing vs. Growth Investing

**[VISUAL: Split screen comparison]**

**Horace:** Now let's compare dividend investing to growth investing. These are often thought of as opposites — but they don't have to be.

**Stella:** Growth investors focus on capital gains — buying stocks they expect to rise in price. Dividend investors focus more on income.

**Horace:** But here's the thing — total return includes both price appreciation and dividends. A steady dividend payer can actually outperform a growth stock over the long run when you factor in reinvestment.

**[VISUAL: Chart comparing $10,000 investment — dividend vs. growth over 30 years]**

**Stella:** The power of compounding dividends is remarkable. Let me give you an example.

**Horace:** Please do!

**Stella:** Say you invest $10,000 in a stock with a 4% yield, and the dividend grows 5% per year. If you reinvest all dividends, after 30 years you'd have significantly more than someone who just held a non-dividend stock with similar price growth.

**Horace:** And importantly — dividends provide income even when the market is down. That's huge for psychological stability.

**[VISUAL: Graph showing dividend income smoothing out market volatility]**

### Section 4: Income Strategies Using Dividends

**[VISUAL: Strategy overview cards]**

**Stella:** So how do investors actually build income from dividends? Let's look at a few strategies.

**Horace:** **Strategy 1: High-yield portfolio**. You focus on stocks or ETFs with above-average yields — maybe 4–6% or higher.

**Stella:** The risk here is dividend traps. You need to do your homework — check payout ratios, balance sheets, and whether the company can sustain those payments.

**Horace:** **Strategy 2: Dividend growth portfolio**. You focus on companies that consistently raise dividends over time — even if the current yield is modest.

**Stella:** This strategy is great for younger investors. You're buying a lower yield today, but as dividends grow, your yield on cost keeps rising.

**[VISUAL: Chart showing yield on cost growth over time]**

**Horace:** **Strategy 3: Dividend ETFs**. If you don't want to pick individual stocks, there are ETFs specifically designed for dividend investing.

**Stella:** Examples include VYM, SCHD, and DVY — each with different approaches to selecting dividend stocks.

**Horace:** **Strategy 4: REITs for income**. Real Estate Investment Trusts — or REITs — are required by law to pay out at least 90% of taxable income as dividends. They can offer very high yields.

**Stella:** REITs give you real estate exposure without actually buying property — and the yields can be quite attractive, often 4–8%.

**[VISUAL: Comparison of dividend strategies]**

| Strategy | Yield Focus | Risk Level | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-yield portfolio | 4–8%+ | Medium-High | Income now |
| Dividend growth | 1.5–3% | Low-Medium | Long-term compounding |
| Dividend ETFs | 2–4% | Low-Medium | Diversification |
| REITs | 4–8% | Medium | Real estate income |

## Glossary

- **Dividend**: A payment made by a company to its shareholders from earnings
- **Dividend Yield**: Annual dividend divided by stock price
- **Payout Ratio**: Percentage of earnings paid as dividends
- **Dividend Aristocrat**: A company that has raised dividends for 25+ consecutive years
- **DRIP**: Dividend Reinvestment Plan — automatically reinvests dividends into more shares
- **REIT**: Real Estate Investment Trust — required to pay 90%+ of income as dividends
- **Ex-Dividend Date**: The cutoff date to be eligible for the next dividend payment
- **Qualified Dividend**: A dividend taxed at the lower capital gains rate
- **Yield on Cost**: Your effective yield based on your original purchase price

### 第一節：什麼是股利？

**[VISUAL: Simple definition card]**

**陳馬：** 那麼，股利到底是什麼？股利是公司將部分盈餘分配給股東的款項——通常以季為單位發放。

**小魚：** 你可以這樣想——當你持有一家公司的股票，你就是這家公司的部分所有人。有些公司會直接把獲利分享給股東，作為回饋。

**陳馬：** 不過，不是所有公司都會發放股利。年輕的高成長公司——像科技新創——往往把所有盈餘再投入業務發展。

**小魚：** 但較成熟的公司——公用事業、民生消費、金融業——通常會定期穩定地發放股利。

**[VISUAL: Table comparing dividend-paying vs. non-dividend companies]**

| 特點 | 配息公司 | 成長股公司 |
|---|---|---|
| 發展階段 | 成熟期 | 早期／成長期 |
| 盈餘用途 | 分配給股東 | 再投入業務 |
| 代表企業 | 可口可樂、嬌生 | 亞馬遜、特斯拉 |
| 吸引力 | 收益 + 穩定性 | 資本利得 |

**陳馬：** 股利通常以現金方式發放，直接存入你的券商帳戶。但有些公司提供所謂的 DRIP——股利再投資計畫。

**小魚：** DRIP 會自動將你收到的股利用來購買同一檔股票的股份——對長期複利效果非常有幫助。

### 第二節：關鍵股利指標

**[VISUAL: Dashboard showing key metrics]**

**小魚：** 來談談數字吧。評估配息股票時，有幾個關鍵指標你一定要掌握。

**陳馬：** 第一個——**股利殖利率**。它告訴你，公司支付的股利相對於股價的比例。

**[VISUAL: Formula card]**

```
股利殖利率 = 每股年度股利 / 股價 × 100%
```

**小魚：** 所以如果一檔股票每年配發 $4 股利，股價為 $100，殖利率就是 4%。

**陳馬：** 很簡單！但這裡有個陷阱——殖利率過高，其實可能是警示訊號。

**小魚：** 沒錯。如果股價大幅下跌而股利維持不變，殖利率看起來很高——但公司可能已經出問題了。這就叫做**股利陷阱**。

**[VISUAL: Illustration of dividend trap with falling stock price]**

**陳馬：** 第二個指標——**配發率**。這顯示盈餘中有多少比例作為股利發放。

**[VISUAL: Formula card]**

```
配發率 = 每股股利 / 每股盈餘 × 100%
```

**小魚：** 配發率低於 60% 通常被視為健康。超過 80%？那可能就難以為繼了。

**陳馬：** 第三個關鍵指標——**股利成長率**。股利是否逐年提升？

**小魚：** 持續提高股利的公司被稱為**股利貴族**——他們已連續 25 年以上調升股利。

### 第四節：運用股利的收益策略

**[VISUAL: Strategy overview cards]**
