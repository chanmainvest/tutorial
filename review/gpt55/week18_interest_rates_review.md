# Review: course/week18_interest_rates.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 18 is a strong macro-mechanics lesson. The "discount rate as universal denominator" frame is powerful, and the rate cascade chart is the right visual foundation. The lesson does a good job linking bonds, mortgage rates, credit spreads, equity duration, growth/value rotation, and the 2022 regime shock.

The lesson needs a little more nuance around what the Fed controls, what credit spreads represent, and which claims are date-stamped projections versus durable teaching. It also has another next-week mismatch.

## Content Critique

- The opening is excellent. Students need to feel that discount rates touch every asset.
- The Fed/market distinction is useful, but "the Fed sets one rate" is too simplified. The Fed targets a range for fed funds and also controls/administers tools such as IORB, ON RRP, and the discount window. The broad teaching point is right, but nuance improves credibility.
- The rate cascade is very useful. Add that BAA spreads reflect default risk, liquidity risk, downgrade risk, and risk appetite, not pure default probability.
- The Gordon growth example is excellent for making duration intuitive.
- The 2022 long-duration tech explanation is strong, but separate the Treasury-rate move from equity-risk-premium expansion and earnings-expectation changes.
- The real-rate section is important. The 2008 negative breakeven should not be presented as the single reason for QE; it was one alarm among deflation risk, market dysfunction, and the zero lower bound.
- The 1981-2020 regime framing is SOUL-aligned, but "long bonds beat the S&P 500" should be sourced or softened because it depends on instrument, start/end dates, and total-return assumptions.
- The claim that passive index investing's success is built on the 1981-2020 bond bull should be nuanced. Passive's advantage also comes from low fees, tax efficiency, broad diversification, and active-manager underperformance.
- The 2022-2024 hiking-cycle chronology is useful, but future/current facts such as April 2026 need date-stamping and should be easy to revise.
- The growth-versus-value duration section is strong, but utilities should not be grouped as a rate-benefiting short-duration value sector. Utilities are often bond-like and rate-sensitive.
- The inflation-causation claim in the Q&A (60% supply / 40% demand) is too precise without a source.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- The YouTube outro says Week 19 is inflation, but Week 19 is corporate finance in the course map. Inflation is a side lesson.
- The course already covered inflation/cycle material in Week 10 and Side 6; make sure this lesson points to the right next file.
- The passive-regime argument should be aligned with SOUL.md without over-attributing passive success solely to falling rates.

## Presentation Improvements

- Add a box: "What the Fed controls directly versus what markets price."
- Add a spread decomposition visual for mortgage and BAA corporate yields.
- Add a duration ladder: cash, 2-year, 10-year, 30-year, value equity, growth equity.
- Add a real-rate versus nominal-rate mini-chart.
- Add a rate-shock checklist for portfolios: bonds, tech, REITs, banks, mortgage affordability, dollar.
- Add source notes for all live/historical 2026 chronology claims.

## YouTube Script Critique

The script is compelling and likely to retain viewers because it links a dry topic to asset-price pain. The slider call-to-action is excellent.

Specific improvements:

- Let Stella ask: "If the Fed does not set mortgage rates, why do mortgage rates move on Fed day?"
- Add a quick distinction between expected and surprise rate moves.
- Soften "bonds beat stocks for 40 years" or cite the exact long-bond total-return series.
- Fix the Week 19 preview.
- Add one practical viewer action: check the 10-year Treasury, 10-year real yield, and breakeven before interpreting any Fed headline.

## Interactive Demo Feedback

The referenced `course/interactive/week18_rate_impact.html` is exactly the right tool. Discount-rate, growth, and cash-flow sliders should make present-value sensitivity unforgettable.

Suggested improvements:

- Add guardrails when `r <= g`, because Gordon growth becomes infinite/invalid.
- Add real versus nominal rate mode.
- Add duration estimate and convexity estimate for each cash-flow stream.
- Add sector examples: banks, utilities, software, REITs, long Treasuries.
- Add rate path scenarios rather than only a one-time rate change.

## New Interactive Demo Ideas

- Equity-duration calculator.
- Mortgage affordability shock simulator.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The best entertainment device is the slider reveal: same business, same cash flows, different discount rate, dramatically different price. Build the video around that tactile shock.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson helps students make money by preventing them from owning several assets that are secretly the same rate bet. A tech-heavy portfolio, a long-bond ETF, and a REIT sleeve can all be long-duration bets in disguise.

## SOUL Consistency Flags

- Strong alignment with SOUL principles #2, #5, #6, #7, #12, and #17.
- Needs care not to reduce passive indexing's success solely to the bond bull market.
- Needs cleaner connection to SOUL's regime-reversal triggers: rates, inflation, fiscal dominance, passive-flow fragility, and volatility regime.
