# Review: course/week02_index_funds_etfs.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 2 is strong, practical, and closer to the course's intended identity than the overview pages. It teaches the orthodox Boglehead answer clearly, then gives the SOUL-consistent caveat that passive has worked for forty years but may not work forever. The lesson also has a sharp retail-investor protection angle: fees, advisors, insurance wrappers, and product incentives. That is exactly the kind of material that can help students keep more of their money.

The main weaknesses are cross-border tax practicality, geographic-allocation consistency, and occasionally over-broad claims. For a course aimed at English, HK, TW, and CN readers, recommending US-listed ETFs through Interactive Brokers without discussing dividend withholding, US estate tax exposure, local tax treatment, and Irish-domiciled UCITS alternatives is a major gap. It can change the correct product choice for non-US investors.

## Content Critique

- The opening is excellent: it gives beginners a simple action while still warning that the simple answer is regime-conditional.
- "If you stop reading after Week 2... you will outperform the vast majority of investors" is motivating and data-backed, but add a caveat: provided you hold for decades, keep costs low, avoid panic selling, and choose products appropriate to your tax jurisdiction.
- The active-vs-passive data is useful. Add a citation note or date for the SPIVA figures so the lesson does not look like using approximate numbers as permanent facts.
- The ETF vs mutual fund section is clear, but it needs a note that for US retirement accounts the wrapper decision is often made by the plan, and low-cost mutual funds can be perfectly fine.
- The advisor compensation section is valuable and protective. It should include one practical sentence: fee-only fiduciary does not mean free of all conflicts; AUM fees can still create incentives to keep assets under management instead of paying down debt or buying real estate.
- The insurance section has the right warning, but "almost all scams" and "over any 20-year window" may be too sweeping. Keep the warning but distinguish between bad investment wrappers, legitimate risk-transfer insurance, estate-planning cases, and jurisdiction-specific tax uses.
- The "Funds You Actually Need" table is practical, but it should include non-US investor caveats. HK/TW/SG investors buying US-listed ETFs may face US dividend withholding and potential US estate tax. Irish-domiciled UCITS ETFs may be more tax-efficient for some non-US residents.
- The buying instructions suggest market orders. For highly liquid ETFs during normal US market hours, that is usually fine, but beginners should learn to avoid market orders outside regular hours and use limit orders for less liquid ETFs.
- The three-fund portfolio section is good because it frames BND as a textbook starting point with a shelf life. This directly improves the Level 1 bond-shock-absorber issue.
- The international allocation discussion needs SOUL alignment. The lesson presents 70% US / 30% international and VXUS/VT as normal defaults, but SOUL principle #16 says the US market is effectively the global market, HK/CN equities are uninvestable for Horace, and Taiwan is mostly TSMC plus a few world-class tech names. The lesson should teach the orthodox global-market-cap view, then explicitly state Horace's disagreement.
- The statement "Two ETFs is roughly 11,000 stocks across every major economy on earth — there is nothing left to diversify into at the equity level" is too broad. It ignores currency, factor, sector, valuation, governance, tax, liquidity, and geopolitical risks. It also conflicts with the course's later argument that global equities are increasingly synchronized.

## Presentation Improvements

- Add a "US investor vs non-US investor" decision box before listing tickers.
- Add a "What problem does this product solve?" column to the ETF table: US core, total US, ex-US, world, bond ballast, tech tilt.
- Add a "Do not buy this from a salesperson" checklist: front load, surrender charge, 12b-1 fee, opaque insurance wrapper, product brochure emphasizing downside protection without showing cap/participation/fee mechanics.
- The lesson is long. Add a summary after each major block: index mechanics, active failure, fee drag, product traps, actual implementation, passive caveat.
- Add a student worksheet: select brokerage, identify tax residency, compare VOO/VTI/UCITS alternatives, calculate fee drag, choose an auto-invest rule.

## YouTube Script Critique

The script has a good title and strong cold open. "$700,000. That's what your fees cost you" is more clickable than a generic ETF explainer. The script also has a strong retention loop: Horace says index ETFs are the answer, then promises the honest caveat at the end.

Specific improvements:

- The 30-minute runtime is justified by the content, but it should be chaptered aggressively. Each major section can become a timestamp and a short clip.
- Add more Stella skepticism around the passive cliffhanger: "If you think passive might break, why are you telling me to buy it?" That question is central and would improve trust.
- Add a cross-border Stella question: "What if I live in Hong Kong or Taiwan and use Interactive Brokers? Is VOO still the right ticker?" This would make the channel feel more tailored to the actual audience.
- The insurance segment is likely to get comments from agents. Preempt the backlash: "If you sell insurance and think I am being unfair, show the all-in fees and net illustrated return."
- The Bogle history segment is good, but could use more emotional contrast: Wall Street called it mediocre because Wall Street needed investors to believe genius was rentable.
- Consider cutting one or two active-manager examples in the main video and moving them to a sidebar or short. Buffett, Lynch, Medallion may be enough for retention.
- The ending line "The index works until it doesn't" is strong. Pair it with a visual of foundation versus upper floors so viewers understand they should not skip the simple action.

## Chart And Visual Feedback

Existing visuals are useful:

- SPIVA underperformance chart should be central and animated.
- Expense-ratio drag chart is one of the highest-value visuals in the course so far.
- Buy-term-and-invest-the-difference chart is useful, but it must show assumptions clearly because insurance comparisons are assumption-sensitive.
- Mermaid diagrams are fine in markdown, but some should become polished static images for the public site and YouTube.

Recommended upgrades:

- Fee drag calculator chart with sliders for starting amount, annual contribution, gross return, fee, and years.
- ETF concentration chart showing top-10 S&P 500 weight over time, not just current concentration.
- ETF overlap heatmap: VOO, VTI, QQQ, VT, VXUS, BND. This would help students avoid false diversification.
- Advisor incentive flow chart: investor dollars -> advisor -> fund company -> revenue sharing -> ongoing fees.
- Passive tailwind timeline: demographics, falling rates, globalization, Fed put, passive flows.
- US-listed ETF versus UCITS ETF tax comparison for non-US investors.

## Interactive Demo Ideas

- Active-vs-passive simulator: choose fee, turnover, tax rate, alpha assumption, and see how much alpha is needed to beat VOO after costs.
- Expense drag calculator: input portfolio size, contribution, time horizon, and product fee; output dollar cost of the fee.
- ETF picker decision tree: US taxpayer / non-US taxpayer, taxable / retirement account, need US-only / global / bond / tech tilt, then produce candidate product categories, not personalized advice.
- ETF overlap explorer: enter common tickers and see overlapping top holdings and effective concentration in Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, etc.
- Advisor conflict quiz: read an advisor pitch and identify conflicts, loads, surrender charges, AUM incentives, and fiduciary gaps.
- Passive-regime dashboard: sliders for demographics, real rates, valuation concentration, and passive-flow share; output a qualitative risk level for passive indexing.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The lesson already has strong villains: high fees, conflicted advisors, opaque insurance products, and false genius. Keep those, but avoid making the tone feel like pure industry rage. The entertainment should come from revealing the mechanism: "Here is the boring ETF; here is why the giant industry hates that it works."

The best recurring bit could be Stella bringing a product pitch and Horace dismantling the fee structure. That format would be both educational and highly watchable.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson has direct money value. It can save students enormous amounts by steering them away from high-fee funds, product sales, and unnecessary complexity. To make it even more useful, add a jurisdiction-aware implementation layer and teach students to calculate after-tax, after-fee outcomes rather than stopping at ticker selection.

## SOUL Consistency Flags

- Good alignment with SOUL principle #2: passive has worked for 40 years but is not permanent.
- Good alignment with SOUL principle #14: index funds are a beginner default, not the advanced final portfolio.
- Potential conflict with SOUL principle #16: broad international ETF recommendations should be presented as orthodox diversification first, then Horace's US-centric disagreement should be stated clearly.
- The lesson should avoid implying equity diversification across countries is complete diversification when later SOUL guidance says global markets are largely synchronized to US liquidity.
