# Review: course/week20_earnings_cashflow.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 20 is practical and important. It teaches students to look past EPS, understand accruals, read earnings quality, and connect cash flow to valuation. The anomaly framing, red flags, and company examples make the lesson useful for actual stock analysis.

The lesson needs technical corrections around free-cash-flow definitions and accrual formulas. It also contains broken Week 21 links. The headline "earnings are an opinion, cash is a fact" is memorable, but the lesson should keep emphasizing that cash-flow presentation can also be managed through classification, factoring, supplier finance, and working-capital timing.

## Content Critique

- The opening hook is strong, but use the slogan carefully. Cash is harder to fake than EPS, not impossible to manipulate.
- The PEAD and accruals anomaly framing is useful and sophisticated.
- The lesson should source or soften claims like "market reaction is only two thirds of what it should be."
- The Sloan balance-sheet accrual formula is simplified and should be corrected. The classic version adjusts current assets for cash and current liabilities for short-term debt/taxes payable, then subtracts depreciation.
- The statement `FCFF = OCF - Capex` is too loose. Under US GAAP, operating cash flow generally includes interest paid, so `OCF - Capex` is often closer to levered free cash flow or free cash flow after interest. True FCFF usually adjusts EBIT/NOPAT plus D&A minus working capital and capex, or adds back after-tax interest.
- The FCFE formula should be harmonized with the chosen FCFF definition. If using CFO-based FCF, say explicitly whether interest is already included.
- The "adjusted FCF" warning is excellent, especially around stock-based compensation.
- The five red flags are practical and should stay.
- The accrual ratio `(Net income - OCF) / Net income` can become unstable when net income is small or negative. Prefer scaling by average assets or revenue in screening.
- The late-cycle 2026 EPS-versus-FCF dispersion section is useful, but date-stamp and source it.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- Links to `week21_valuation_dcf.md` appear broken. The actual course file is `week21_equity_valuation.md`.
- The YouTube outro links to the same wrong Week 21 filename.
- The lesson says cash-versus-accruals interpretation is a structural alpha source. This is defensible, but it should be connected to SOUL's formal alpha list rather than introduced as if it were already explicitly named there.
- The Week 19 link is correct.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The best entertainment is forensic: show Stella finding a company that looks profitable on EPS but fails the cash-flow checks. Let the viewer feel like they learned a detective skill.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson is highly money-useful. Avoiding high-accrual, cash-poor, adjusted-metric-heavy companies is a real edge for retail investors because it prevents avoidable blowups and improves watchlist quality.
