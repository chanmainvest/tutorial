# Review: course/week19_corporate_finance.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 19 is strong and investor-facing in the right way. It avoids turning corporate finance into a CFO textbook chapter and instead asks the right shareholder questions: how is the business financed, does ROIC beat WACC, is capital returned intelligently, and is M&A creating or destroying value?

The main improvements are nuance around M&A funding, current cost of debt, and tax-specific buyback/dividend claims. The lesson is otherwise coherent and fits well after interest rates and before earnings/cash flow.

## Content Critique

- The opening is excellent: owning a share means hiring management to allocate capital.
- The debt-versus-equity example is clear and mathematically useful.
- The Modigliani-Miller section is good because it treats M&M as a baseline, not a real-world prescription.
- The WACC section is clear, but Q1 should use current market yield on outstanding debt or comparable debt, not only the most recent bond issuance, because old issuance yields can be stale.
- Add that WACC can include preferred stock or leases for some companies; the simplified formula is fine but should be labeled simplified.
- ROIC > WACC is correctly presented as the cleanest capital-allocation test.
- The buyback section is excellent. Share-count reduction as silent compounder is a high-value concept.
- The buyback-versus-dividend tax discussion is US-specific. Add a jurisdiction caveat because the course has non-US readers.
- The stock-based-compensation dilution offset trap is one of the best practical points in the lesson.
- The M&A section is useful but a little too categorical. Target shareholders usually win and acquirer shareholders often underperform, but deal type, valuation, payment mix, and acquirer stock valuation matter.
- The funding-mix checklist needs nuance. All-cash is not always best if it over-levers the acquirer or burns strategic liquidity. All-stock can be attractive for the acquirer if its own shares are genuinely overvalued; all-stock is worst when the acquirer is issuing undervalued shares.
- The phrase "the day-one reaction in the acquirer's stock is usually directionally right" should be softened. Announcement reactions are informative, not infallible.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- The next-week preview to earnings quality and free cash flow correctly matches Week 20.
- The lesson's US-tax comments should be aligned with Week 12's need for jurisdiction-specific implementation guidance.
- The buyback/tax section should not assume US taxable treatment in translations or non-US reader contexts without a caveat.

## Presentation Improvements

- Add a one-page capital allocation scorecard: ROIC-WACC spread, debt/EBITDA, interest coverage, buyback discipline, share count, M&A record.
- Add a worked WACC example using current market values.
- Add a "buyback good/bad/neutral" decision tree.
- Add an M&A checklist card with premium, multiple, funding, synergies, integration risk, management record.
- Add a five-year share-count chart exercise.
- Add a warning box on stock-based compensation and adjusted EPS.

## YouTube Script Critique

The script is clear and practical. The Apple buyback section is likely to hold attention because it turns an abstract denominator into a visible compounding engine.

Specific improvements:

- Let Stella ask why all-stock deals are not always bad. Horace can explain overvalued versus undervalued acquirer stock.
- Add one example of a bad buyback where share count did not fall.
- Add a practical homework assignment: pick one holding and check diluted shares outstanding over five years.
- Use less categorical language on M&A; make default skepticism the stance, not automatic selling.
- Add a non-US tax caveat in the dividend/buyback discussion.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The lesson can be entertaining through CEO scorekeeping. Make students feel like owners judging capital allocation, not spectators reacting to press releases.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson is very money-useful. Avoiding serial acquirers, dilution-offset buybacks, and ROIC-below-WACC growth can save students from superficially attractive but value-destructive stocks.
