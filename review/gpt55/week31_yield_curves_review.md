# Review: course/week31_yield_curves.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 31 is one of the stronger fixed-income lessons. It gives students a practical map for reading the yield curve through level, slope, curvature, forwards, regimes, and retail-relevant bond ETF expressions. The Horace-view section is also useful because it connects SOUL's regime-weighting philosophy to an observable market input.

The lesson should be tightened before publication. It overstates the recession-prediction record, treats forwards too much like clean market expectations before fully caveating term premium, has some curve-trade expression issues, and needs clearer labeling for April 2026 data and interactive assumptions.

## Content Critique

- The level/slope/curvature framework is excellent and appropriately professional.
- The par/spot/forward explanation is useful, but it should say forwards are implied no-arbitrage rates plus risk premia, not pure expected future spot rates.
- The line "upward-sloping curve says the market expects the short rate to rise" is too strong. A normal curve can also reflect positive term premium with flat or falling expected short rates.
- "Inversions have predicted every US recession since 1970" should be softened. The specific curve measure matters, timing varies, false positives/long lags exist, and 2019/2020 is complicated by COVID.
- The 2022 inversion discussion is good, but if this lesson is anchored in April 2026, it needs a clear source/date note and a brief explanation of whether the expected recession did or did not arrive.
- Credit-spread heuristics in §1 should be labeled as regime-dependent. IG and high-yield spreads are not always one-to-two and four-to-six points.
- The Litterman-Scheinkman PCA content is strong, but percentages should be described as approximate and sample-dependent.
- The four curve regimes are clear, but the asset playbooks need more caveats. Bull steepeners can arrive during recession stress, which can be bad for equities despite lower rates.
- The Horace-view section aligns with the course's SOUL, but "the recession lands in that window" is too deterministic. Make it a base-rate and sizing signal rather than a prediction.
- The butterfly example says "long $50M 5-year, short $25M 2-year and $25M 30-year, weighted by DV01." Equal notionals are not necessarily DV01 neutral; either show DV01 weights or keep it conceptual.
- The statement that pensions/insurers create persistent richness at both the long end and very short end is plausible but oversimplified. Add supply/demand and balance-sheet caveats.
- Q4's ETF trade examples need precision. A long TLT / short IEF position is not a clean long-end steepening trade in the usual yield-curve sense.
- Q8 says a bull steepener is typically good for equities, while §2.4 says equities are mixed. The latter is more accurate because bull steepeners often occur when growth is slowing.
- Q9's claim that the forwards-vs-dot-plot gap is a reliable contrarian signal is too strong. It is a useful disagreement indicator, not a reliable trading signal.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- Week 30 previewed Week 31 as IV rank/vol regimes, but Week 31 is actually yield curves.
- Week 31's own Week 32 preview appears plausible, but should be checked when Week 32 is reviewed.
- The lesson uses "retail investor" more consistently than earlier files, which is good.
- If the course uses static images generated by scripts, the April 2026 curve images need source/date labels to distinguish live data from constructed examples.

## Presentation Improvements

- Add a small "forwards are not forecasts" box immediately after the forward-rate formula.
- Add a chart callout distinguishing 10y-2y and 10y-3m recession signals.
- Add a timeline graphic: inversion, peak inversion, uninversion, recession-window distribution.
- Add a regime table row for "what can go wrong" in each bull/bear steepener/flattener case.
- Add a practical checklist: what a monthly retail investor should record from the curve.
- Add source notes for April 2026 yields, ACM term premium, and PCA percentages.

## YouTube Script Critique

The script is strong and more polished than several earlier weeks. It has a clean hook, a compact three-glance framework, and a good bridge from macro chart to portfolio behavior.

Specific improvements:

- Avoid saying the curve "predicted every recession" without a curve-measure and timing caveat.
- When explaining forwards, say "implied path plus term premium" every time the word forecast appears.
- Add a Stella challenge: "If it inverted in 2022 and stocks rallied, how useful was it?" That question would improve credibility and retention.
- The Horace regime-dial section is valuable but long; break it with a simple visual of equity sleeve versus tail-hedge sleeve weights.
- The outro is accurate and aligns with Week 32.

## Chart And Visual Feedback

Existing visuals are conceptually strong:

- Current/historical curve comparison.
- 10y-2y slope history.

Recommended upgrades:

- Add a source/date caption to the April 2026 curve.
- Add shaded recession bands to the slope-history chart if not already present.
- Add a forward-curve mini chart.
- Add a PCA factor illustration showing level/slope/curvature moves on the same curve.
- Add an asset-sensitivity heatmap: cash, short Treasuries, long Treasuries, banks, growth stocks, cyclicals.

## Interactive Demo Feedback

The `week31_curve_builder.html` interactive is useful and well matched to the lesson. It lets users drag tenors and see shape, slope, curvature, and 1y1y forward update live.

Suggested improvements:

- Label the forward calculation as approximate because it interpolates a 1-year point from 3-month and 2-year yields.
- Clarify that slider yields are treated like spot rates for the formula even though quoted Treasury yields are usually par yields.
- Add preset source/date notes.
- Add a toggle for 10y-2y versus 10y-3m slope.
- Add a term-premium slider to show why forwards are not pure forecasts.
- On mobile, five slider columns may feel cramped; consider a 2-column or stacked layout.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The most watchable moment is the 2022 inversion paradox: the curve screamed recession while equities rallied for a long time. Let Stella call that out. Horace can then explain why signals are not stopwatches, which makes the lesson more trustworthy.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson can help students avoid the classic mistake of treating all bond funds as the same. The money-making usefulness is in understanding when cash, short duration, long duration, and tail hedges each deserve capital.

## SOUL Consistency Flags

- Strong alignment with SOUL's regime-aware allocation and Dragon/tail-hedge thinking.
- Needs to keep the orthodox view first: yield curve as probabilistic macro input, not deterministic recession trigger.
- The Horace-view allocation language should stay framed as a weight adjustment, not a mechanical trading rule.
