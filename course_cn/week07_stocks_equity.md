# Week 10, Lesson 3: Preferred Stock and Hybrid Securities

## Overview
This lesson explores preferred stock and hybrid securities — financial instruments that blend features of both stocks and bonds. By the end of this lesson, students will understand what preferred stock is, how it differs from common stock and bonds, and when it might be appropriate for different types of investors.

---

## Script

**[VISUAL: Title card — "Preferred Stock and Hybrid Securities"]**

**Horace:** Welcome back, everyone! Today we're diving into something that sits right in the middle — preferred stock. It's not quite a stock, and it's not quite a bond. It's a hybrid.

**Stella:** And hybrid securities are super interesting because they have features from both worlds. As an investor, understanding these instruments can really expand your toolkit.

**Horace:** Exactly. Let's start with the basics. What is preferred stock?

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side comparison — Common Stock vs Preferred Stock vs Bond]**

**Stella:** Preferred stock is a class of ownership in a company that has a higher claim on the company's assets and earnings than common stock.

**Horace:** The word "preferred" here refers to getting paid first. If a company pays dividends, preferred shareholders get their dividend before common shareholders. And if the company goes bankrupt, preferred shareholders get paid before common shareholders — though still after bondholders.

**Stella:** Think of it like a priority queue. Bondholders are first. Preferred shareholders are second. Common shareholders are last.

**[VISUAL: Priority queue diagram — Bondholders → Preferred Shareholders → Common Shareholders]**

**Horace:** So why would someone choose preferred stock over a bond? Great question.

**Stella:** A few reasons. First, preferred stock often has a higher yield than bonds from the same company. That extra yield compensates for the extra risk — because preferred shareholders get paid after bondholders.

**Horace:** Second, some preferred dividends are "qualified," meaning they're taxed at a lower capital gains rate rather than ordinary income rate. That can be a big deal for certain investors.

**Stella:** And third, some preferred stocks are convertible — they can be converted into common stock at a set ratio. So you get some upside potential too.

**[VISUAL: Three icons — Higher Yield, Tax Advantage, Convertibility]**

**Horace:** Now let's talk about the different types of preferred stock. There are several flavors.

**Stella:** The most basic is straight preferred — also called non-cumulative preferred. You get a fixed dividend, but if the company skips a payment, you don't get to make it up later.

**Horace:** Then there's cumulative preferred. If the company skips a payment, those missed dividends accumulate. The company must pay all accumulated dividends before paying anything to common shareholders.

**[VISUAL: Cumulative vs Non-Cumulative Preferred Dividend Chart]**

**Stella:** There's also participating preferred, which is less common. These shareholders can receive extra dividends beyond the fixed rate if the company does well — they "participate" in the company's profits.

**Horace:** And convertible preferred, which we mentioned. You can convert your preferred shares into a fixed number of common shares. This is especially popular in startup and venture capital investing.

**Stella:** Finally, there are callable preferred stocks. The company can "call" — meaning buy back — the preferred shares at a predetermined price after a certain date.

**[VISUAL: Types of Preferred Stock — table with columns: Type, Fixed Dividend, Missed Payments Accumulate, Conversion Option, Company Buyback Option]**

**Horace:** Let's talk numbers. Preferred stock usually has a par value — often $25 or $100. The dividend is usually expressed as a percentage of that par value.

**Stella:** For example, if a preferred stock has a par value of $25 and a 6% dividend rate, the annual dividend is $1.50 per share. That's $25 × 6% = $1.50.

**Horace:** And if you can buy that preferred stock in the market for $24, your actual yield is a bit higher — $1.50 / $24 = 6.25%.

**[VISUAL: Calculation — Par Value × Dividend Rate = Annual Dividend → Market Yield = Annual Dividend / Market Price]**

**Stella:** Now, preferred stock prices behave a lot like bond prices. When interest rates go up, preferred stock prices tend to go down. When interest rates go down, preferred stock prices tend to go up.

**Horace:** This is because preferred stock is primarily a yield instrument. Investors compare its yield to other income-generating instruments. If rates rise elsewhere, the fixed preferred dividend becomes less attractive, so the price drops.

**[VISUAL: Interest Rate vs Preferred Stock Price — inverse relationship chart]**

**Stella:** This interest rate sensitivity is one of the main risks of preferred stock — especially for non-callable preferred with no maturity date. The price can be quite volatile relative to what you paid.

**Horace:** Right. Unlike bonds, most preferred stocks don't have a maturity date. You could hold it forever, or sell it in the market. The market price fluctuates based on interest rates and the company's creditworthiness.

**Stella:** Speaking of creditworthiness — preferred stock is also subject to credit risk. If the company gets into financial trouble, they may suspend the preferred dividend. For non-cumulative preferred, that income is just gone.

**[VISUAL: Risk Summary — Interest Rate Risk, Credit Risk, No Maturity / Perpetual Risk]**

**Horace:** So who actually buys preferred stock?

**Stella:** Historically, the biggest buyers of preferred stock are corporations — specifically for the dividends received deduction, or DRD. Under US tax law, corporations can exclude 50% to 65% of dividends received from domestic corporations from taxable income. So preferred dividends are highly tax-efficient for corporate holders.

**Horace:** Individual investors also buy preferred stock, often in ETFs or directly, seeking higher yield than bonds while accepting more risk. Retirees and income-focused investors are common buyers.

**[VISUAL: Buyer Profiles — Corporations (DRD benefit), Income Investors, ETF holders]**

**Stella:** Let's talk about hybrid securities more broadly. Preferred stock is one example, but there are others.

**Horace:** Convertible bonds are a big one. These are bonds that can be converted into a fixed number of shares at a set price — the conversion price. They pay regular interest, but if the stock price rises above the conversion price, they become quite valuable.

**[VISUAL: Convertible Bond diagram — Bond Floor + Equity Upside]**

**Stella:** The bond floor is the minimum value — what the bond would be worth if it never got converted. The equity upside is the additional value from the conversion option.

**Horace:** There are also contingent convertible bonds — called CoCos — used mainly by European banks. These convert to equity or get written down if the bank's capital ratio falls below a threshold. They're designed to automatically recapitalize banks during stress.

**Stella:** Another hybrid is trust preferred securities, or TruPS. These were popular with banks before the 2008 financial crisis. They combined features of debt and equity in a way that let banks count them as capital.

**[VISUAL: Hybrid Securities Spectrum — from Pure Debt to Pure Equity, with Convertible Bonds, Preferred Stock, CoCos labeled]**

**Horace:** The takeaway on hybrids is that they exist on a spectrum between pure debt and pure equity. The more equity-like features they have, the higher the potential return — and the higher the risk.

**Stella:** And the more debt-like features, the more predictable the cash flows — but usually lower potential returns and still exposed to credit risk.

**Horace:** For most individual investors, preferred stock shows up most commonly through ETFs — like preferred stock ETFs that hold baskets of preferred from large financial companies.

**Stella:** These ETFs provide diversification, liquidity, and professional selection. But remember — they still carry interest rate risk and credit risk.

**[VISUAL: Example ETF — showing hypothetical holdings of preferred stock from banks and utilities]**

**Horace:** Alright, let's wrap up. Preferred stock is a hybrid security that sits between common stock and bonds.

**Stella:** It offers a fixed dividend, priority over common shareholders, and sometimes tax advantages or conversion rights.

**Horace:** But it comes with risks — interest rate sensitivity, credit risk, and potentially no maturity date.

**Stella:** Understanding where preferred stock fits on the debt-equity spectrum helps you make smarter decisions about when — and whether — to use it in your portfolio.

**Horace:** That's it for today. See you in the next lesson!

## Key Concepts

- **Preferred stock** is a hybrid security with priority over common stock for dividends and in liquidation.
- **Cumulative preferred** accumulates missed dividends; **non-cumulative preferred** does not.
- **Convertible preferred** can be converted into common stock at a fixed ratio.
- **Callable preferred** can be repurchased by the company at a set price.
- Preferred stock prices are sensitive to **interest rates** — like bonds.
- **Hybrid securities** include convertible bonds, CoCos, and trust preferred securities.
- The main buyers of preferred stock are **corporations** (for the dividends received deduction) and **income investors**.

本课探讨优先股与混合证券——融合了股票和债券双重特性的金融工具。学完本课后，学生将理解优先股的含义、它与普通股和债券的区别，以及它在什么情况下适合不同类型的投资者。

**陳馬：** 欢迎回来，大家好！今天我们要深入探讨一个处于中间地带的概念——优先股。它不完全是股票，也不完全是债券，而是一种混合体。

**小魚：** 混合证券非常有意思，因为它兼具两个世界的特征。作为投资者，理解这些工具真的能大大拓展你的投资工具箱。

**陳馬：** 没错。我们先从基础开始。什么是优先股？

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side comparison — Common Stock vs Preferred Stock vs Bond]**

**小魚：** 优先股是公司的一种所有权类别，相比普通股，它对公司资产和盈利享有更高的优先求偿权。

**陳馬：** "优先"这个词指的是优先获得偿付。如果公司派发股息，优先股股东会在普通股股东之前收到股息。如果公司破产，优先股股东也会在普通股股东之前获得偿付——但仍排在债券持有人之后。

**小魚：** 把它想象成一个优先级队列：债券持有人排第一，优先股股东排第二，普通股股东排最后。

**[VISUAL: Priority queue diagram — Bondholders → Preferred Shareholders → Common Shareholders]**

**陳馬：** 那么，为什么有人会选择优先股而不是债券呢？这是个好问题。

**小魚：** 原因有几个。第一，优先股的收益率通常高于同一公司的债券。这个额外的收益率是对额外风险的补偿——因为优先股股东的偿付顺序排在债券持有人之后。

**陳馬：** 第二，某些优先股息是"合格股息"，意味着它们按较低的资本利得税率征税，而非普通所得税率。对某些投资者来说，这非常重要。

**小魚：** 第三，部分优先股是可转换的——可以按固定比例转换为普通股，因此也具备一定的上行潜力。

**[VISUAL: Three icons — Higher Yield, Tax Advantage, Convertibility]**

**陳馬：** 现在来聊聊优先股的不同类型，它有好几种。

**小魚：** 最基本的是直接优先股，也称为非累积优先股。你可以获得固定股息，但如果公司跳过一次派息，你事后无法补回那次股息。

**陳馬：** 然后是累积优先股。如果公司跳过派息，未支付的股息会累积起来。在向普通股股东派发任何款项之前，公司必须先支付所有累积的股息。

**[VISUAL: Cumulative vs Non-Cumulative Preferred Dividend Chart]**

**小魚：** 还有参与优先股，这种比较少见。这类股东除了固定股息外，如果公司业绩出色，还可以获得额外股息——他们"参与"公司的利润分配。

**陳馬：** 还有我们提到过的可转换优先股。你可以将优先股份按固定数量转换为普通股，这在初创企业和风险投资领域尤为流行。

**小魚：** 最后是可赎回优先股。公司可以在特定日期后以预定价格"赎回"——也就是买回——优先股。

**[VISUAL: Types of Preferred Stock — table with columns: Type, Fixed Dividend, Missed Payments Accumulate, Conversion Option, Company Buyback Option]**

**陳馬：** 来看看具体数字。优先股通常有票面价值，一般为25美元或100美元。股息通常以票面价值的百分比表示。

**小魚：** 举个例子，如果一只优先股的票面价值为25美元，股息率为6%，则年度股息为每股1.50美元，即25美元 × 6% = 1.50美元。

**陳馬：** 如果你能在市场上以24美元买入该优先股，你的实际收益率会略高——1.50美元 ÷ 24美元 = 6.25%。

**[VISUAL: Calculation — Par Value × Dividend Rate = Annual Dividend → Market Yield = Annual Dividend / Market Price]**

**小魚：** 优先股的价格走势和债券非常相似。当利率上升时，优先股价格往往会下跌；当利率下降时，优先股价格往往会上涨。

**陳馬：** 这是因为优先股本质上是一种收益率工具。投资者会将其收益率与其他创收工具进行比较。如果其他地方的利率上升，固定的优先股息就变得不那么吸引人，因此价格会下跌。

**[VISUAL: Interest Rate vs Preferred Stock Price — inverse relationship chart]**

**小魚：** 这种对利率的敏感性是优先股的主要风险之一——尤其是没有到期日的不可赎回优先股。相对于你的买入价，价格可能波动相当大。

**陳馬：** 对。与债券不同，大多数优先股没有到期日。你可以永久持有，也可以在市场上出售。市场价格会随利率和公司信用状况的变化而波动。

**小魚：** 说到信用状况——优先股同样面临信用风险。如果公司陷入财务困境，他们可能会暂停优先股息。对于非累积优先股，那笔收入就永远消失了。

**[VISUAL: Risk Summary — Interest Rate Risk, Credit Risk, No Maturity / Perpetual Risk]**

**陳馬：** 那么，优先股的实际买家是谁？

**小魚：** 从历史上看，优先股最大的买家是企业——主要是为了获得股息收入扣除（DRD）。根据美国税法，企业可以将从国内企业获得的股息中的50%至65%从应税所得中扣除。因此，优先股息对企业持有者来说税务效益极高。

**陳馬：** 个人投资者也会购买优先股，通常通过交易所交易基金或直接购买，寻求比债券更高的收益率，同时承担更多风险。退休人员和注重收益的投资者是常见的买家群体。

**[VISUAL: Buyer Profiles — Corporations (DRD benefit), Income Investors, ETF holders]**

**小魚：** 我们来更宏观地聊聊混合证券。优先股是其中一个例子，但还有其他品种。

**陳馬：** 可转换债券是一大类。这类债券可以按固定数量转换为股份，转换价格是预先设定的。它们支付定期利息，但如果股票价格升至转换价格以上，其价值就会变得相当可观。

**[VISUAL: Convertible Bond diagram — Bond Floor + Equity Upside]**

**小魚：** 债券底价是最低价值——即债券在永远不被转换的情况下的价值。股权上行空间则是来自转换期权的额外价值。

**陳馬：** 还有或有可转换债券——即CoCos——主要被欧洲银行使用。当银行的资本充足率降至某个门槛以下时，这些债券会转换为股权或被减记。它们旨在在压力时期自动为银行补充资本。

**小魚：** 另一种混合证券是信托优先证券（TruPS）。这类证券在2008年金融危机前很受银行欢迎。它们以一种能让银行将其计入资本的方式结合了债务和股权的特征。

**[VISUAL: Hybrid Securities Spectrum — from Pure Debt to Pure Equity, with Convertible Bonds, Preferred Stock, CoCos labeled]**

**陳馬：** 关于混合证券，核心要点在于它们存在于纯债务和纯股权之间的一个谱系上。股权特征越多，潜在收益越高——风险也越高。

**小魚：** 债务特征越多，现金流就越可预测——但潜在收益通常较低，且仍面临信用风险。

**陳馬：** 对于大多数个人投资者来说，优先股最常见的参与方式是通过交易所交易基金——例如持有大型金融公司优先股篮子的优先股交易所交易基金。

**小魚：** 这类交易所交易基金提供分散投资、流动性和专业筛选。但请记住——它们仍然承担利率风险和信用风险。

**[VISUAL: Example ETF — showing hypothetical holdings of preferred stock from banks and utilities]**

**陳馬：** 好了，我们来总结一下。优先股是一种介于普通股和债券之间的混合证券。

**小魚：** 它提供固定股息、对普通股股东的优先权，有时还具有税务优势或转换权利。

**陳馬：** 但它也存在风险——对利率敏感、面临信用风险，且可能没有到期日。

**小魚：** 理解优先股在债务-股权谱系中的位置，有助于你做出更明智的决策——判断何时以及是否应该将其纳入你的投资组合。

## 核心概念

- **优先股**是一种混合证券，在股息派发和清算时优先于普通股。
- **累积优先股**会累积未支付的股息；**非累积优先股**则不会。
- **可转换优先股**可以按固定比例转换为普通股。
- **可赎回优先股**可由公司以约定价格回购。
- 优先股价格对**利率**敏感——与债券类似。
- **混合证券**包括可转换债券、CoCos和信托优先证券。
- 优先股的主要买家是**企业**（为获得股息收入扣除）和**注重收益的投资者**。
