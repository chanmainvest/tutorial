# Review: course/week16_sector_rotation.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 16 is strong. It teaches sector rotation without selling it as easy money, and the heatmap-versus-cycle-map contrast is exactly the right way to make the lesson honest. The best line is that the cycle map is a statistical tendency, not a calendar.

The lesson needs a few factual and consistency fixes: Visa/Mastercard sector classification, possible backfilled histories for XLC/XLRE, and another wrong next-week preview. It should also make the "sector conviction tranches" section safer, because small-cap options/speculative names can tempt students into oversized bets.

## Content Critique

- The opening is compelling. The 2022 sector-spread example makes the point immediately.
- The balance is good: sector rotation is a real alpha source, but most retail rotation loses.
- The eleven-sector table is useful, but the Visa/Mastercard classification appears wrong. Under current GICS, payment processors like Visa and Mastercard belong to Financials, not Technology.
- The lesson correctly warns that XLC and XLRE have shorter histories. If the heatmap shows 2010-2024 data for them, clarify whether it uses live ETF data, GICS backfill, or synthetic/spliced history.
- The canonical cycle map is useful and orthodox.
- The 2020 example is excellent because it shows why the map can be right for ten weeks and wrong for the calendar year.
- The momentum-versus-mean-reversion section is practical. Add more precision on rebalance frequency and transaction/tax costs.
- The retail-rotation P&L section is one of the lesson's strongest parts. It protects students from performance chasing.
- The sector-tranche expression is interesting, but options on small caps and pre-revenue names should be framed as advanced/speculative, not a natural next step for most readers.
- The April 2026 sector tilt comments should be date-stamped and clearly separated from permanent teaching.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- The YouTube outro says next week is the small-cap premium, but Week 17 is performance metrics.
- The script says last week was about correlation between asset classes. Week 15 was multi-asset allocation, so this is close enough, but it should be phrased more precisely.
- The lesson uses "retail traders" once; repo guidance prefers retail investors.
- The US-only sector stance should be reconciled with Week 12's VXUS allocation and Week 15's international-equity caveat.

## Presentation Improvements

- Add a sector-classification footnote for non-intuitive names.
- Add a data-methodology note for XLRE and XLC before their inception.
- Add a small-tilt example: 90% SPY, 5% XLV, 5% XLP versus a full sector bet.
- Add a signal stack: yield curve, PMI, claims, Fed policy, sector leadership, credit spreads.
- Add a "retail rotation failure loop" diagram: chase winner -> drawdown -> sell -> buy next headline sector.
- Add a tax wrapper note: rotate in tax-advantaged accounts if rotating at all.

## YouTube Script Critique

Specific improvements:

- Add a quick correction to sector classification examples.
- Let Stella try the naive strategy in the lab and be disappointed by the P&L.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

The entertainment is the map getting humbled. Show the beautiful textbook map, then slam it with 2011, 2020, and 2022. That is memorable and honest.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson helps students make money by limiting the damage from naive rotation. A modest 10-15% tilt can express a view without turning the whole portfolio into a sector bet.
