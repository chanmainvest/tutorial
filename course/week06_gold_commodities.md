# Week 6: Gold and Commodities — Stores of Value vs Cashflow Assets

---

## Part 1: Reading Section

---

### 1. Why This Is Important

Every textbook portfolio chapter eventually arrives at the same
awkward question: should you own gold? And usually the answer is
either an evangelical yes (the goldbug answer) or a dismissive no
(the Bogleheads answer). Both are wrong because both are arguing
about the wrong thing. Gold is not a stock. It is not a bond. It
does not produce cash flow, will never pay you a dividend, and its
"intrinsic value" is — as J.P. Morgan put it in 1912 — exactly the
same as the intrinsic value of every other store of value:
**consensus**.

You need to think clearly about gold and commodities for four
reasons.

1. **They are the cleanest test of what "store of value" actually
   means.** Stocks compound earnings. Bonds compound coupons. Gold
   compounds nothing. If gold is worth holding, the reason cannot
   be cashflow — it has to be the durability of belief that other
   humans will accept it as money in fifty years. That same logic
   applies to fiat dollars, to bitcoin, and to every currency that
   has ever existed. Gold is the laboratory for thinking about
   belief-priced assets.
2. **The inflation-hedge claim is half true and half marketing.**
   Gold has crushed inflation in three windows (the 1970s, the
   2000s, the 2020s) and gone nowhere for decades in between. If
   you do not understand *when* the hedge works, you will buy at a
   peak and sell at a trough — exactly what the average gold ETF
   investor has done since 2011.
3. **Industrial commodities — oil, copper, wheat — are not the same
   asset class as gold and should not be in the same mental
   bucket.** They are inputs to production, mean-revert toward the
   marginal cost of supply, and roll-cost via futures contango will
   eat the long-term holder alive. Owning USO is not "owning oil";
   it is owning a slow bleed that loses to spot oil even when oil
   itself doubles.
4. **The implementation choice is huge.** GLD/IAU vs futures-based
   ETFs vs miners vs bullion are four very different exposures
   with four very different tax, fee, and tracking profiles. Per
   Horace's US-only frame (SOUL #16), GLD or IAU is the
   investable answer for a US-listed retail book; everything else
   is a niche.

This lesson covers what gold actually is, when its inflation hedge
has worked, why industrial commodities are a different beast, and
the small set of US-listed instruments that retail investors should
consider.

### 2. What You Need to Know

#### 2.1 Gold Has No Cashflow — Its Value Is Belief

Stocks are claims on future earnings. Bonds are claims on future
coupons. The discounted-cashflow framework that values both of them
does not apply to gold because gold has no cashflow to discount.
The price of gold is whatever the marginal buyer is willing to pay
the marginal seller, and the marginal buyer's willingness to pay
is set entirely by their belief that the next marginal buyer will
pay at least as much.

This is not a slur. It is the same logic that prices the US dollar.
A hundred-dollar bill produces no cashflow either. Its value is
that the next person down the chain will accept it for $100 of
goods. Gold's belief is 5,000 years old. The pure-fiat dollar's
belief, in its current post-Bretton-Woods form, is younger than
the Beatles. Bitcoin's belief is younger than the iPhone. As Horace
puts it, **every store of value rests on belief**, and the only
honest question is how durable the belief has been and how durable
it is likely to remain.

The practical implication of "no cashflow" is that gold cannot
compound in the way equities do. A diversified equity index
delivered roughly 7% real per year for a hundred years because
companies kept producing more goods, paying more dividends, and
buying back more stock. Gold, over the same hundred years,
delivered roughly 1.0–1.5% real — slightly above inflation. It is
*money that holds its value*; it is not capital that grows.

#### 2.2 The Inflation-Hedge Claim — When It Holds, When It Doesn't

The marketing claim is "gold hedges inflation." The historical
record is more specific: **gold hedges sharp, persistent, and
poorly-anticipated inflation, in regimes where real interest rates
are falling**. When real rates are rising (Volcker 1981, the
2010s, briefly 2022 before the bond market broke), gold goes
nowhere or down.

The chart below plots the real (CPI-adjusted) USD gold price from
1971 — the year Nixon closed the gold window and floated the
dollar — through April 2026.

Three things should jump out.

- **Three real peaks at roughly the same level.** The 1980, 2011,
  and 2024–26 highs all sit between $2,200 and $2,500 in 2026
  dollars. That is not a coincidence — it is the marginal demand
  ceiling at which gold becomes "expensive enough to sell" relative
  to other inflation hedges (TIPS, real estate, commodities).
- **Two lost decades.** From 1980 to 1999 the real price fell
  almost 80%. From 2012 to 2018 it lost about 40%. Holding gold
  through those windows tested every goldbug's faith.
- **The rallies cluster around macro stress.** 1971–80: stagflation,
  oil crisis, end of Bretton Woods. 2001–11: dollar weakness,
  GFC, eurozone crisis, rates collapsing toward zero. 2019–26:
  COVID liquidity, post-COVID CPI shock, central bank buying as
  reserve diversification accelerated.

The single most reliable predictor of the gold price is **the
real US 10-year yield, inverted.** Real rates fall, gold rises.
Real rates rise, gold falls. The mechanism is opportunity cost: if
you can earn 2% real on a Treasury, the no-yield asset is
expensive. If you can only earn -1% real on a Treasury, gold's
zero yield suddenly looks competitive.

ran circles around it.

The conclusion is not "gold is bad" or "gold is good." It is that
**gold and equities are different exposures** and a portfolio that
holds a meaningful slice of each will look better in *both* regimes
than either pure portfolio looked across the full half-century. A
small permanent gold allocation — Horace runs gold as a permanent
sleeve in his four-tranche barbell (SOUL #13, #14) — was a free
lunch on the realized data, in the sense that the rebalanced
mixture had a higher Sharpe than either component on its own.

#### 2.4 Industrial Commodities Are Not Stores of Value

Oil, copper, wheat, soybeans, natural gas — these are inputs to
production, not money. Three properties make them very different
from gold:

1. **They mean-revert toward the marginal cost of supply.** When
   oil hits $130 in 2008 or 2022, US shale producers turn on rigs,
   supply rises, price falls back toward the $50–$70 marginal-cost
   band. When oil drops to $30, marginal producers shut in,
   supply falls, price rises. Over a long horizon, an industrial
   commodity does not compound; it oscillates around a slowly
   drifting cost curve.
2. **The real long-run trend is *down*, not up.** Productivity in
   commodity production has historically beaten demand growth. Real
   oil prices in 2026 are roughly the same as in 1973. Real wheat
   prices have *fallen* over a century. The "commodity supercycle"
   thesis is real for individual decades but not for a buy-and-hold
   compounder.
3. **They are highly correlated with each other and with the
   business cycle.** Owning a basket of industrial commodities is
   close to owning a leveraged short on the dollar plus a leveraged
   long on global GDP. It is a factor exposure, not a diversifier.

For US retail investors, the implication is that industrial
commodities are *trades* (positions sized for a specific macro view)
and not *holdings* (permanent allocations). And as Horace
emphasizes (SOUL #15), the cleanest way to hold a tactical
commodity exposure for tax purposes is **via call options on the
relevant equity ETF** — not by owning the futures-based commodity
ETF directly. We unpack that mechanic in §2.6.

#### 2.5 The Contango Trap — Why USO Is Not "Oil"

Most commodity ETFs do not own physical barrels. They own the
front-month futures contract, and they roll it forward each month
into the next contract. When the futures curve is in **contango**
(later contracts more expensive than the front month — the normal
state for oil and most industrial commodities, because storage
costs money), every monthly roll is a small loss: you sell the
expiring contract for $X and buy the next month for $X + roll cost.

The cumulative damage is enormous. The most famous case study is
USO, the United States Oil Fund. Spot WTI crude in early 2026 is
within a few dollars of where it was in early 2010. Over the same
sixteen-year window, USO has lost roughly 80% of its value. The
gap is the contango bleed.

Gold is the exception that proves the rule. GLD and IAU each own
**physical gold bars** in vaults — there is no roll cost, no
contango, and the tracking error to spot gold is small (the only
drag is the management fee, 17–25 bps a year). That structural
difference is why gold is investable as a passive long-term holding
and most other commodities are not.

For a retail US-listed investor running a permanent gold sleeve,
the practical answer is **GLDM (cheapest) for the buy-and-hold
core** with **GLD (deepest options chain) for the position you
intend to overlay covered calls on**. Everything else is a
specialty tool.

The tax angle worth knowing: GLD, IAU, and GLDM are all structured
as grantor trusts holding a "collectible," which means **long-term
capital gains are taxed at the 28% collectibles rate** in the US,
not the standard 15–20% LTCG rate. GDX miners, by contrast, are
ordinary stock and qualify for the standard rates plus qualified
dividends. This is the kind of detail that makes a 5–10% gold
allocation worth holding inside a tax-advantaged account if you
have one — and again, per SOUL #15, it is part of why expressing
the gold thesis through long-dated **call options on GLD** rather
than spot GLD is often more tax-efficient: option exercises on the
trust units inherit the underlying tax treatment, but the option
positions themselves are taxed under section 1256 mark-to-market
in the case of broad-based index options, and as ordinary capital
gains for equity options. The mechanical detail matters.

#### 2.7 Sizing and the Permanent-Allocation Question

So how much gold? Three honest answers.

- **0%** is the Bogleheads answer. It is internally consistent if
  you believe inflation will stay tame and real rates will stay
  positive forever. The 60/40 backtest from 1981–2021 supports it.
  The 2022 result does not.
- **5–10%** is the institutional answer (the Permanent Portfolio
  variants, Bridgewater's All Weather, Horace's four-tranche
  barbell). It is the smallest allocation large enough to matter
  in an inflation regime and small enough not to drag you in a
  benign one. This is the right answer for most US retail
  investors.
- **20%+** is the goldbug answer. It is internally consistent only
  if you have a strong conviction view on monetary regime change.
  Most people who run this allocation have not honestly stress-
  tested what happens if they are wrong for a decade.

The interactive at the end of this lesson lets you slice the
historical record by inflation regime to see when the hedge
actually paid off and when it did not.

### 3. Common Misconceptions

1. **"Gold has intrinsic value."** It doesn't. Neither does the
   dollar, the euro, or bitcoin. All four are belief-priced.
2. **"Gold always rises with inflation."** It rises with *poorly-
   anticipated, sustained* inflation when real rates are falling.
   In a Volcker-style "rates ahead of inflation" regime, gold
   falls.
3. **"Gold is a bad investment because it has no yield."** It is a
   bad *cashflow* asset. As a portfolio diversifier with negative
   real-rate sensitivity, its long-run Sharpe addition to a 60/40
   has been positive in the historical record.
4. **"Owning USO is owning oil."** It is owning a rolling futures
   strategy that bleeds 5–10% a year via contango. Spot oil moves
   are not USO moves.
5. **"Gold miners are leveraged gold."** They are levered to the
   *gold price minus all-in sustaining costs* and carry operational
   risk, country risk, and management risk. GDX has lost to GLD
   over most multi-decade windows despite higher operating
   leverage in theory.
6. **"Bitcoin replaces gold."** Bitcoin has 15 years of belief.
   Gold has 5,000. They may both belong in a portfolio, but the
   substitution claim ignores the durability gap.
7. **"You should own commodities for diversification."** Industrial
   commodities are pro-cyclical risk assets, not diversifiers.
   Gold is the diversifier; oil and copper are factor bets on
   global growth.
8. **"GLD costs the same as buying physical."** GLD has a 0.40%
   annual fee and small bid-ask. Coins from a dealer carry a
   3–6% premium plus storage and insurance. For a paper investor,
   GLD wins. For a doomsday-physical-delivery investor, the
   comparison does not apply.
9. **"The 1980 high was the all-time high."** Not in real terms.
   The 1980 peak ($850 nominal) is around $2,400 in 2026 dollars,
   roughly the same as today. Real-terms thinking changes the
   chart.
10. **"Central banks selling gold means gold is dead."** Central
    banks were *net sellers* from 1990–2008 (gold was weak) and
    have been *net buyers* every year since 2010 (gold has been
    strong). Central bank flow is a price-setting trade, not a
    sign of irrelevance.

### 4. Q&A Section

**Q: If gold has no cashflow, how can I justify owning it?**
A: The same way you justify holding US dollars in a savings
account. You are not holding either for cashflow; you are holding
them as a store of purchasing power and an option on tail-event
demand. Gold has held purchasing power across regimes that
flattened multiple national currencies. That is the value
proposition — and it is small relative to equity compounding,
which is exactly why a 5–10% allocation is a reasonable upper
bound for most investors.

**Q: Is bitcoin a better gold?**
A: Maybe in 50 years; not yet. Bitcoin's belief is 15 years old.
Gold's is 5,000. Bitcoin has higher upside *if* the belief
durables, but the conditional-on-failure outcome is materially
worse than gold's. Running both as small parallel sleeves is more
honest than picking one.

**Q: What is the right benchmark for "did gold work"?**
A: Real interest rates, not nominal CPI. The cleanest one-line
test: did real 10-year yields fall during the period? If yes, gold
should have risen, and if it did, the hedge worked. Use FRED
series DFII10 for a quick read.

**Q: Why does GLD use a 28% collectibles tax rate?**
A: The IRS classifies gold bullion as a collectible regardless of
whether you hold a coin or a unit of a grantor trust that holds
bullion on your behalf. Closed-end funds (CEF, PHYS) have an
optional QEF election that can convert to long-term capital gains
treatment, but it requires annual paperwork and is not the
default. For most US investors, the practical workaround is
holding GLD/IAU/GLDM in an IRA.

**Q: Should I buy physical coins or GLD?**
A: For investment purposes, GLD or GLDM. For doomsday
preparedness, physical with off-site storage. The two are
different products. The premium on coins (3–6%) plus storage
(0.5%/year for an insured vault) plus the bid-ask on resale wipes
out the structural advantage over many years.

**Q: What about gold mining stocks?**
A: GDX is a leveraged-but-noisy gold proxy. It has higher beta to
gold but also picks up operational risk, country risk
(many large miners are in jurisdictions with regime risk),
hedging-program risk, and management risk. Over the long run, GDX
has *underperformed* GLD despite higher operating leverage on
paper. If you want gold exposure, own gold; if you want a tactical
trade on the gold price moving sharply higher, consider GDX as a
short-duration position.

**Q: What is the "central bank buying" story?**
A: Since around 2010, central banks (especially China, Russia,
India, Turkey, and central Asian states) have been net buyers of
gold every year, with the pace accelerating sharply after the
2022 freezing of Russian FX reserves. They are diversifying away
from dollar reserves and into a non-confiscable, sovereign-neutral
asset. This is a structural marginal bid that has supported gold
prices in the 2020s.

**Q: How do I use options on GLD per SOUL #15?**
A: Three common structures: (1) covered calls on existing GLD to
generate income with a price ceiling; (2) cash-secured puts to
accumulate GLD at lower prices; (3) deep-ITM long-dated GLD calls
("LEAPS replacement") to hold gold exposure with less capital
deployed. The tax point is that capital tied up in option premium
is not subject to the 28% collectibles rate while the position is
open — and rolling a long-dated call is a different taxable event
than realizing a long-term gain on the underlying. We cover the
mechanics in detail in Weeks 27 and 38.

**Q: When should I trim my gold sleeve?**
A: When real 10-year yields are above +2% and rising, when central
bank buying slows, and when the gold-to-S&P 500 ratio has compressed
below its 20-year median. None of those conditions hold in April
2026. The "permanent allocation" is permanent; the size is what you
adjust at the margin in extreme regime windows.

**Q: Is there a case for silver?**
A: Silver has a partial monetary identity and a much larger
industrial demand component (solar, electronics). It has higher
volatility than gold and does not behave as a clean store-of-value
hedge. SLV is a tactical instrument, not a permanent one.

**Q: Are oil ETFs ever buyable?**
A: Tactically, around oil-supply shocks where the front-month
contract goes into *backwardation* (front more expensive than
deferred), the roll yield turns positive and a futures-based ETF
can briefly outperform spot. Outside that window, no. The cleaner
expression is options on energy equity ETFs (XLE, XOP), which
sidesteps the contango problem entirely — exactly the SOUL #15
move.

as a passive long-term holding and most other commodities are not.

**[BLOCK F — The US-listed toolkit — 14:00]**

**Stella:** OK so for a US investor — per SOUL principle sixteen,
US-listed only — what should they actually buy?

**Horace:** Three names worth knowing. GLDM at ten basis points is
the cheapest physical-gold ETF. GLD at forty is the most liquid
with the deepest options market. IAU at twenty-five sits between
them. For a buy-and-hold core, GLDM. For anything where you want
to overlay options, GLD.

**Stella:** What about miners?

**Horace:** GDX is leveraged gold with operational and country
risk attached. It looks like gold on paper but has underperformed
GLD across most multi-decade windows. Use it tactically if you
want to express a high-conviction view on the gold price moving
fast, but don't think of it as the same exposure.

**Stella:** Tax angle worth knowing?

**Horace:** Yes — and this is where SOUL principle fifteen comes
in. GLD, IAU, GLDM are all grantor trusts holding "collectibles"
under US tax law. The long-term capital gains rate is 28%, not
the 15-to-20% that applies to ordinary equities. That's a real
drag if you're realizing big gains in a taxable account.

**Stella:** Workarounds?

**Horace:** Two. First, hold gold in an IRA — most retail brokers
allow GLD and IAU inside a Roth or traditional IRA, and the 28%
rate doesn't apply inside a tax-deferred wrapper. Second — and
this is the SOUL principle fifteen play — express the gold thesis
through long-dated *call options* on GLD instead of holding the
spot units. Equity options are taxed as capital gains, not as
collectibles. Long-dated deep-ITM calls on GLD give you most of
the spot exposure with a different tax treatment, and they free
up capital you can deploy elsewhere.

**[BLOCK G — Sizing — 15:30]**

**Stella:** How much gold?

**Horace:** Three honest answers. Zero is the Bogleheads answer.
Five to ten percent is the institutional answer — Permanent
Portfolio variants, Bridgewater All Weather, my own four-tranche
barbell. Twenty percent or more is the goldbug answer. Most
investors should be in the five-to-ten range.

**Stella:** Why not zero?

**Horace:** Because the 60/40 backtest from 1981 to 2021 supports
zero, and the 2022 result does not. The regime that made bonds
the diversifier in a 60/40 cracked. Gold was the diversifier in
2022 — flat to up while everything else fell. That's the regime
gold gets paid for.

**Stella:** Why not twenty?

**Horace:** Because then the 1980-to-2000 lost-decade scenario
becomes a portfolio-level disaster. Twenty percent of your book in
an asset that loses eighty percent of its real value is a
career-ending hole. Five to ten percent at the same scenario is a
manageable drag.
