# Review: course/week10_economic_cycles.md

## Overall Assessment

Week 10 is conceptually important and mostly strong. It teaches students that the economy has phases, that indicators have timing, and that asset-class leadership changes by regime. The leading/coincident/lagging hierarchy is especially useful, and the yield-curve section is exactly the kind of macro tool retail investors can realistically monitor.

The lesson's main risk is overconfidence. Cycle awareness is valuable, but the text sometimes sounds like a reliable timing system. The course should make macro useful without implying a beginner can beat passive portfolios just by knowing one cycle fact. The other issue is recurring cross-reference and terminology drift around tranches/barbells and next-week previews.

## Content Critique

- The opening is strong, but "the cycle decides which asset wins" should be softened. The cycle influences expected returns; valuation, policy, liquidity, positioning, and shocks also matter.
- The lesson says the US has had thirteen recessions since 1945, but the table lists twelve. Correct the count or add the missing recession.
- The NBER section is excellent. It clearly explains why waiting for confirmation is too late.
- The Kitchin/Juglar/Kondratieff section is useful if handled carefully, but Kondratieff language can sound like cycle mysticism. Keep the disclaimer that these are intuition layers, not deterministic clocks.
- The leading/coincident/lagging hierarchy is one of the best parts of the lesson. It should become a repeated macro framework in later weeks.
- The yield-curve section is valuable, but the "called every recession" claim needs caveats. The 2019 inversion before COVID is not a normal cycle call, and 10y-2y versus 10y-3m have different track records.
- The chart starts in 1976 while the text claims every recession since 1955. Either expand the chart/data or align the claim to the chart window.
- The asset-performance-by-regime framework is useful, but the tagging method needs explanation. Students will be confused if 2008 is treated as late-cycle in one sentence and recession in another.
- "Phase awareness is worth four times the equity risk premium" in the script is too strong without a precise calculation and implementation assumption.
- The 2026 live setup is useful, but date-stamp it clearly and expect it to age quickly.
- The Fed-put misconception is very good: the Fed can support markets when inflation is controlled, but cannot always rescue risk assets during inflation shocks.

## Cross-Reference And Consistency Issues

- The lesson says there are thirteen recessions since 1945 but lists twelve.
- The reading section says "the four tranches in the framework include a barbell of equities and long Treasuries plus a real-asset sleeve." This appears to mix SOUL's four-tranche sector-cycle model with barbell/asset allocation language.
- Q9 says the barbell and four-tranche structure exist mostly for retirees. SOUL.md frames the barbell as Horace's advanced portfolio shape, not primarily a retiree tool.
- The YouTube outro says next week is inflation, but Week 11 is behavioral biases.

## YouTube Script Critique

The script has strong teaching energy and good pacing. The "folklore versus trading desks" framing is clickable. The danger is that it overpromises.

Specific improvements:

- Replace "If you only know one thing about the cycle, you outperform a passive 60/40" with a more defensible hook: "One cycle signal can keep you from using the wrong playbook."
- Make Stella challenge the timing problem: "If the yield curve leads by 6 to 24 months, what do I actually do today?"
- Use the 2007/2008/2009 interactive walkthrough as the emotional center. It makes regime change tangible.
- Add a disclaimer that cycle awareness is for gradual tilts, not binary market exits.
- Fix the next-week preview.
- Put the live 2026 discussion in a clearly labeled "as of recording" segment.

## Interactive Demo Feedback

The promised `course/interactive/week10_regime_explorer.html` is exactly the right idea: click a year, see its regime tag, one-year-forward asset returns, and same-regime averages. That makes regime shifts tangible.

Suggested improvements:

- Add methodology notes for how each year is tagged.
- Add confidence/ambiguity labels. Some years are not cleanly one regime.
- Add a toggle for nominal versus real returns.
- Add a date-stamped live dashboard mode separate from historical mode.
- Add a path view: select a sequence such as 2007-2009 or 2020-2022 and show regime transitions year by year.
- Add warnings against using single-year tags as trading signals.

## New Interactive Demo Ideas

- Yield-curve lead-time explorer: select inversion dates and see time to recession, stock returns during lead time, and returns after un-inversion.
- Macro dashboard builder: choose indicators and see whether they are leading, coincident, or lagging.
- Cycle tilt simulator: compare passive 60/40 versus gradual regime tilts under different false-positive costs.
- Accumulator/retiree response planner: same macro signal, different recommended action based on risk capacity.
- Fed-put conditionality simulator: inflation below target versus above target and how policy response changes asset behavior.

## Make It More Entertaining Without Watering It Down

Make the cycle feel like a weather map, not a prophecy. The viewer should learn to check conditions before sailing, not believe they can command the weather. That framing keeps the lesson useful and avoids overpromising.

## Money-Making Usefulness

This lesson helps students make money by preventing them from using the wrong portfolio playbook in the wrong macro regime. The practical rule should be gradual tilting: stop adding risk, lengthen or shorten duration, add real assets, or raise cash depending on signal clusters. Avoid binary calls.
